The scent of burnt ozone and solder has always felt more like home than any traditional living room. Since I was seven, my weekends were spent hunched over my grandfather’s workbench, navigating a labyrinth of resistors and capacitors.

This is where I learned that failure isn't just an option; it's a necessary diagnostic tool. When my first attempt at building a FM radio resulted in a puff of smoke instead of jazz, I didn't feel defeated. I felt curious. Why did the circuit fail? Where was the short?

As I grew, so did the complexity of my projects. What began as simple radios evolved into autonomous drones and low-cost environmental sensors for my local community. However, the most profound lesson didn't come from a successful flight, but from a collaborative disaster during the 2023 Regional Robotics Finals.

Our team’s lead programmer had to leave suddenly, and we were left with a half-written codebase and a lot of stress and confusion among the group members about what we should do next to win the competition.

I stepped into the gap, not because I was the best coder, but because I understood the underlying architecture of our bot. I realized that leadership in a technical environment isn't about having all the answers—it’s about asking the right questions and creating a space where every sub-system—both mechanical and human—can function optimally.
